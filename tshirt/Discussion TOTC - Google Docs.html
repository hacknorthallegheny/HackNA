A Tale of Two Cities by Charles Dickens 
 
Biographical and Historical Context: 
Charles Dickens (1812-1870) is a foremost representative novelist of 
the Victorian Era, a great storyteller and social reformer. This novel 
has always been one of his most popular and beloved novels. Always 
interested in the interaction between individuals and society, Dickens 
was particularly inspired by Thomas Carlyles history. The French 
Revolution. He saw similarities between the forces that led to the 
Revolution and the oppression and unrest he saw occurring in England 
in his own time. Although he supported the idea of people rising up 
against tyranny, the violence that characterized the French Revolution troubled him. In the preface to his novel he 
says, to add something to the popular and picturesque means understanding that terrible time. The story is set in 
London, Paris, and the French countryside at the time of the French Revolution. The book is sympathetic to the 
overthrow of the French aristocracy but highly critical of the reign of terror that followed. 
 
Questions to Consider: 
1. The most recurrent criticism of this novel is that the characters do not have the psychological depth or development 
of other Dickensian figures. Is this fair? Does Sydney Carton's transformation undermine this claim?  
  
2. How does Dickens reconcile his distaste for the Revolution with his identity as a social crusader? Does he believe in 
the people's right to revolt under an oppressive government? 
  
3. What do you infer is the author's idea of the ideal woman? Compare the adherence to traditional gender roles by 
Lucie Manette and Madame Defarge. Is Dickens constrained by literary or social conventions, for example by making 
a manly woman the villain and a feminine woman the sentimental heroine? 
  
4. Almost every character in the novel has something to hide. How do these secrets influence the story? 
 
5. Although the idea of revenge is very appealing, the act of revenge can turn the avenger into a worse monster than 
the original wrongdoer. From your reading, does Dickens agree with this statement? 
 
6. Discuss the theme of duty (responsibilities) vs. desire. How does Dickens feel about it and what examples does he 
put in the book? Which characters choose duty and why? Do any choose desire? How does it affect them? How does 
Dickens want the reader to feel about each? 
 
7. Examine how Dickens portrays the mob mentality in the scenes depicting the storming of the Bastille, Darnays 
trials, and the Carmagnole. What do you think he is saying about the human propensity towards evil and forgiveness? 
Cite incidents in the novel to support this theme that love is stronger than hate. 
 
8. Examine the theme of duality in each: Dr. Manette, Sydney Carton, Jerry Cruncher, and Monsieur Defarge. 
  
9. Does the plot's reliance on fate and coincidence make the story less believable or less powerful? 
 
10. Examine the theme of resurrection in the novel. Which characters are brought back to life and how? Is there any 
situation from which resurrection is impossible? 
11. What kinds of endings now seem most satisfying to us? Are happy endings always unrealistic? Are sad or unhappy 
endings always depressing? 
 
12. What are the advantages and disadvantages of serial publication? Why isnt used more often today?
